## 5. CLEAN TRANSCRIPT

**Dane Frederiksen:** Hey everybody. My name is Dane Frederiksen. I am a B2B video expert, and I'm here today with Todd Fairbairn, who is from Emotiv Mobility. We're here to talk about what's going on in this crazy world of marketing and AI and all the things we need to be thinking about right now. Todd, why don't you introduce yourself and tell us a little bit about what we need to know about you.

**Todd Fairbairn:** Dane, thank you so much for having me on. Todd Fairbairn — I am Head of Marketing and Brand at Emotiv Mobility. It's a really interesting time to be in our space. B2B marketing is always a challenge. You've got to cut through the noise. But I think you throw in AI and everything that's happening, and for brand folks on usually smaller teams, you've got to leverage AI. But how do you do it in a way that's authentic, true to the brand, and isn't just slop? It's a great tool, but you've got to be responsible with it.

**Dane:** It's such a great moment to be having this conversation. AI just took all the air out of the room. We all went nuts last year, made all this slop — word of the year. Now I'm seeing the pendulum swing back the other way. They're hiring content writers to be human again. There's finally some appreciation for the work that we do, and it has real value beyond just hitting numbers with volume. So when that meets actual practice — how are you using AI, or not using it? What's top of mind for you?

**Todd:** We use it a fair bit, in full transparency. It's not our North Star, nor does it help us get there. But we use it to gut check a lot of our activities. My marketing team — we're a smaller B2B in the automotive space. It's myself, I have a marketing performance and operations manager who leads our campaigns and activities, and then I have a content creator who's diving into the material.

AI for us — we use it for keeping our website content fresh, looking at what others in the space are doing, and guiding us on blog posts, thinking about keywords and things of that nature. We'd love to see how we can use tools from a video content perspective. That is real intriguing for a smaller group. How can we get more with less? But then use it with the diligence and responsibility of remaining true to the brand.

**Dane:** This is one of my goals with this interview series — to take the chocolate and the peanut butter and put them together. Me as a video expert, you as a marketer with your expertise in your niche. I'm always advocating for leveraging video in an authentic way that still lets you use AI responsibly without diluting your brand.

I think the best term for it is video-first content marketing. The idea is that video, because it contains all other formats, is the perfect raw ingredient for a content system that's repeatable and ongoing. Imagine an interview like this chopped up into shorts, take the transcript, use that for written content. If you plan that ahead of time rather than repurposing as an afterthought — I call it pre-purposing. Planning ahead, building a system that's low-lift.

SMEs shouldn't be writing articles, especially in this day and age. An interview like this is a great way to extract on-brand thought leadership and repurpose it across all channels. There's this idea that so many different levels of quality are acceptable now. A scrappy iPhone video or a webcam video like this has value if the content is valuable and it's used in the right way. Of course, polished brand videos and Super Bowl commercials have their place too. But post-pandemic, social media world — there's this other lane that B2B is just now waking up to.

**Todd:** I love where your head's at, Dane. Sometimes you never have enough time to tackle these ideas. From your perspective, it's almost like a grand campaign where you have longer form content — whether it's a white paper or a blog — and you break it down and leverage it across a multitude of different facets. That was a big driving principle while I was at Ford Motor Company and we tackled a lot of our marketing communications.

And what I think is so fun — you mentioned the guy running and creating his own content — for so long, B2B has been kind of rigid, no personality. There's a hesitancy for brands, especially because they're just one phase in a bigger end product, to step out and be their own identity. There's a lot of hiding. It's the people, the humanity, that makes a huge impact as a brand differentiator.

It's one of the things we've been looking at doing. How might we get the individuals on an assembly line at one of our facilities to have a TikTok where you're capturing the actual pieces coming together — an activity that 99% of people wouldn't necessarily be interested in, right? But there are so many supply chain companies like ours touching that product. It's really cool moments. Tap into that humanity. The biggest opportunity for a lot of brands in our space is to flex about the people who are doing the work, the individuals contributing to the betterment of the product.

**Dane:** Everyone's sitting on a content goldmine, because you do have the subject matter experts, the people doing the work. There's this idea of hiding behind white papers and ghost writing, all prim and proper and buttoned up. That stuff matters too. But the marketplace has started to demand something different. A lot of execs who want to keep that buttoned-up perspective — they're being asked to take off the mask and be real. And it makes a lot of people uncomfortable.

So what I'm getting closer to now is finding ways people can say yes. The grand master plan content strategy just sounds too big, too hard. How can we start small? Let's give this a pilot project name. Let's call this a test. Making it so the bar is low and you can get some reps in. A lot of people don't want to be on camera. If you can make that less scary — let's just record something and not post it. We'll look at it, talk about it, and then maybe take another swing.

**Todd:** I think there are paths forward in either direction. I'm of the opinion — the beauty of my job is I get to try different things. People in my space haven't done it before. Doesn't mean it's wrong. Doesn't mean it's right. You don't really know until you give it a whirl.

Making this a piece of some grandiose annual strategic marketing plan that's going to deliver X results by 2030 — that just sounds daunting and not fun. Everybody in marketing and branding, we're in it to build things we love to see. You look around to see who's raising the bar, who's trying different things.

I love incremental approaches. Let's try this. We flirted with the idea of TikTok while at CES. That was interesting. It probably helps from a talent-bringing perspective. I don't know how we're structured to support that in perpetuity. But when we were at CES, that's where the audience was. So it would be prudent to be there.

You have to give things the opportunity before you automatically dismiss them. Everything we've known has kind of flipped on its head in the past 10 years. That's the beauty and the frustration of our space.

**Dane:** How are you selling this "we've got to just try it" mentality to people who need ROI, numbers, results? How do you balance the need to measure with the need to test things you can't fully predict?

**Todd:** It is a balancing act, and that's the most stressful part. We'll do little pilot-type programs where it's a piece of a smaller underlying budget. We let the core piece of the business carry the mail, then we see what kind of reaction these little pilots cause. If you string a couple together over a couple of quarters, then you might be on to something. Then it's easier to convince executive leadership that this is something we try a little bit more in subsequent years.

I wish I had piles of money to try different things. But what would be the fun in that? It's these incremental, anecdotal tries to see where we might be able to connect. If we're on to something, then we keep pushing until we crack the egg or start from scratch.

**Dane:** As you're looking forward — what are the things in front of you? What's the next thing for you, or for the industry, your peers? Where are we headed?

**Todd:** I think AI, in terms of being able to harness it — one of my marketing leads has been all over Claude for the past couple of weeks, figuring out how to leverage it better for what we're doing. Really just a compare and contrast to the output we're seeing from other AI tools.

I do think there's going to continue to be a movement to have people telling the story, focusing on individuals to write copy, because there are differences in what you read. You can tell things are AI-written versus an actual individual. That delicate balance will continue to be there.

It's been a wild 12 months. It's probably going to be a wild 24 months as these tools continue to get smarter. We as marketers have a responsibility to be good stewards of the tools, leverage them in a way that's beneficial for our craft. It's exciting to see what will continue to come out of it. Whether it's HubSpot or other platforms pushing AI functionality — you're just inundated with AI everywhere. That'll be fascinating to see how these capabilities all connect and intertwine.

**Dane:** It's the wild west all over again. Todd, thanks for joining me today. Great conversation. Good luck out there.

**Todd:** Absolutely. Dane, appreciate the time. Cheers.
